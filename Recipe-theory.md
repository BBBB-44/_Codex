**How to write a recipe**

A recurring issue I encounter when following recipes is the inefficiency of how information is typically organized. I often find myself wondering whether alternative structures could reduce cognitive load and improve workflow. For example, grouping ingredients by their physical storage location—pantry, refrigerator, freezer—could streamline retrieval and minimize unnecessary movement. Similarly, organizing steps according to a “soiling logic” might reduce cleanup: measuring dry ingredients before wet ones preserves the usability of tools (since reversing the order often forces the use of additional utensils and increases washing). Even commonly recommended practices such as pre-portioning ingredients (mise en place) raise questions—while they may improve execution clarity, they can also generate more items to clean, potentially offsetting their benefits.

In this context, a well-structured recipe should not only list components but encode an efficient process. At minimum, it should specify ingredients, required tools, procedural steps, expected output, and contextual notes. However, beyond these basics, the organization of information should be intentional: ingredients could be grouped by storage or usage sequence, tools by function and reuse, and tasks ordered to optimize flow, minimize friction, and reduce unnecessary cleanup. Such an approach reframes the recipe from a static set of instructions into a designed system aimed at efficiency, clarity, and practical usability.

**Workflow-oriented recipe design**

What I am attempting to develop does not belong to a single, formally named culinary method, but rather emerges at the intersection of several established domains concerned with efficiency, cognition, and structured action. At its core, this approach aligns with the notion of Workflow, in which tasks are deliberately organized to optimize execution, and with Process optimization, which seeks to minimize wasted time, motion, and resources. The attention given to sequencing actions, reusing tools, and reducing unnecessary cleanup closely parallels principles found in Lean manufacturing, particularly the systematic elimination of inefficiencies.

At the same time, this perspective is informed by considerations drawn from Cognitive load, as the structuring of a recipe can either alleviate or exacerbate the mental effort required during execution. By reducing decision points, clarifying sequences, and externalizing constraints (such as tool states), the recipe becomes easier to follow in real time. In this sense, it also reflects concerns central to Human factors and ergonomics, where systems are designed to align with human capabilities and limitations. Finally, the overall structure resembles a form of Algorithm design, in which ordered steps, resource constraints, and state-dependent operations are carefully orchestrated. Taken together, this approach reframes the recipe not as a static set of instructions, but as an optimized workflow shaped by interdisciplinary principles.

## Origin of the Approach

This approach emerged directly from practical experience in fast-paced food environments, including fast food service, restaurant kitchens, and food assembly contexts. In these settings, repetition, time pressure, and high turnover of tasks revealed that inefficiencies were rarely related to culinary knowledge itself, but rather to how information and actions were structured. Small frictions—such as unnecessary movement between stations, poorly sequenced prep steps, or avoidable dish usage—accumulated into significant cognitive and physical load over the course of a shift.

In attempting to improve personal performance within these environments, I began informally restructuring my own workflow: grouping actions by location, anticipating tool reuse, and reordering tasks to reduce unnecessary transitions and cleanup. Over time, these adaptations revealed a consistent pattern: performance improved not through doing more, but through organizing better. This observation gradually extended beyond the kitchen itself and toward recipes as information systems.

From this perspective, traditional recipes appeared under-optimized for real execution conditions. They often prioritize culinary narrative or ingredient listing conventions over operational flow. The approach developed here therefore originates from a practical attempt to bridge that gap—translating on-the-ground operational constraints into a structured, workflow-oriented way of designing recipes that better reflects how tasks are actually performed under pressure.

# A Systems-Oriented Guide to Writing Better Recipes

Most recipes focus on *what* to do. Stronger recipes also optimize *how the process unfolds under real conditions*—limited time, divided attention, and minimal cleanup.

This guide shows how to design recipes as efficient workflows rather than static instructions.

---

## 1. Use Dual Organization: Storage + Sequence

Avoid choosing between ingredient lists that are easy to gather *or* easy to follow during cooking—use both.

* **Storage view (for gathering):**
  Group ingredients by most likely location (pantry, fridge, freezer)

* **Sequence view (for execution):**
  Reorder ingredients based on when they’re used in the process

This eliminates the tradeoff between efficient prep and smooth execution.

---

## 2. Make Tool State Explicit

Track not just *which* tools you use, but their condition.

* Indicate when tools must stay **clean** or **dry**
* Reuse tools intentionally to avoid unnecessary washing
* Add notes like:

  * “Use the same bowl”
  * “Keep measuring cup dry”
  * “Do not wash yet”

This reduces friction and prevents mid-process decisions.

---

## 3. Treat Cleanup as Part of the Workflow

Cleanup isn’t separate—it’s part of the system.

* Group messy steps together
* Insert **micro-cleaning moments** (e.g., while something simmers)
* Avoid unnecessary prep that increases dish usage

Think of cleanup as something to **schedule**, not postpone.

---

## 4. Apply Conditional Mise en Place

Preparing everything in advance isn’t always optimal.

Use this rule:

* **Pre-measure** ingredients that are:

  * Time-sensitive
  * Easy to mess up
* **Handle inline** ingredients that are:

  * Flexible
  * Low-risk

This reduces both cognitive load and dish count.

---

## 5. Design Modular, Atomic Steps

Each step should be small, clear, and self-contained. Include:

* **Action** – what to do
* **Tool** – what to use
* **State** – what condition matters

Example:

* “Measure 1 cup flour (dry measuring cup, keep dry for reuse)”
* “Add milk using the same cup only after dry ingredients are complete”

This structure improves clarity and prevents errors.

---

## 6. Add a Flow Summary

Start with a quick overview of the process:

**Gather → Prep → Cook → Finish → Clean**

This helps you mentally simulate the workflow before starting.

---

## Key Idea

A well-designed recipe isn’t just instructions—it’s a **workflow optimized for movement, attention, and resource use**.

When done well, cooking feels smoother, faster, and more deliberate—with fewer interruptions and less cleanup.
