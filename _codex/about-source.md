### On the Epistemology and Fragility of “Source” in Digital Knowledge Systems

---

#### 1. Introduction: What do we mean by “source”?

In academic and epistemic practice, a *source* is traditionally understood as a stable referent: a document, artifact, dataset, or testimony that grounds a claim in something retrievable and verifiable. Within classical scholarship, the integrity of knowledge depends on traceability—arguments are expected to be anchored in citational chains that allow independent verification.

However, in digital environments, the ontology of “source” becomes unstable. A URL is not a source in the classical sense; it is a *pointer* to a source that may or may not persist. This introduces a structural fragility: knowledge becomes dependent not only on truth conditions but also on infrastructural persistence.

Thus, we must distinguish:

* **Epistemic source**: the underlying informational object (paper, dataset, article, observation)
* **Digital pointer**: the URL or identifier referencing that object
* **Access state**: whether the object remains retrievable at a given time

The collapse of any one of these layers can disrupt the integrity of citation.

---

#### 2. The problem of persistence: link rot and epistemic decay

The concern you raise—*no guarantees that content will remain available in the future*—is not anecdotal; it is a well-documented phenomenon in information science known as **link rot**.

Studies across academic publishing show that a significant proportion of web citations decay over time, either through:

* Domain expiration
* Content restructuring
* Paywalling or removal
* Institutional archiving failure
* Legal takedown or policy changes

This produces what might be called **epistemic decay**, where the evidential infrastructure of knowledge erodes even when the underlying claims remain valid.

---

#### 3. The illusion of permanence in digital citation

Modern digital scholarship often implicitly treats URLs as stable bibliographic objects. This is an inherited assumption from print culture, where citation targets (books, journals) were materially persistent.

However, digital objects behave more like *processes* than artifacts. A blog post is not a fixed object but a dynamically hosted state within a changing system of servers, permissions, and platforms.

Therefore, citing a URL is less like referencing a book and more like referencing:

> a momentary configuration of a system that may or may not reappear in identical form.

This undermines the classical notion of reproducibility in epistemology.

---

#### 4. The epistemic consequence: distributed, unanchored knowledge

We are increasingly operating in what might be called a **post-appropriative epistemic regime**, characterized by:

* Aggregation over authorship
* Synthesis over originality
* Temporal accumulation over fixed citation chains

In such systems, knowledge becomes **distributed and recombinant**, assembled from fragments that may themselves lack stable provenance.

This does not necessarily imply intellectual irresponsibility. Rather, it reflects a shift in how information is processed: from linear argumentation to networked recomposition.

---

#### 5. Why some researchers “don’t bother with source” (and what this really means)

The statement “I don’t bother with sources” can be interpreted in multiple ways:

##### (a) Practical skepticism about persistence

If sources are unstable, excessive citation becomes performative rather than functional.

##### (b) Epistemic realism about retrieval

If content may vanish, then citation does not guarantee verification—only a historical intention of verification.

##### (c) Shift from validation to synthesis

In some contexts, the goal is not to prove each element, but to construct a coherent synthesis from heterogeneous inputs.

However, it is important to distinguish between:

* **Abandoning sources entirely**
  vs.
* **Acknowledging that sources are probabilistic and fragile**

The former undermines epistemic accountability; the latter reframes it.

---

#### 6. A more robust model: citation as redundancy, not guarantee

A more resilient epistemic model treats sourcing not as a promise of retrieval, but as **redundant anchoring**:

* Archival links (e.g., web archives)
* DOI-based systems
* Cross-citation in multiple independent repositories
* Summarization alongside reference
* Versioned snapshots rather than live URLs

In this model, citation is not meant to guarantee permanence, but to increase *probabilistic recoverability*.

---

### 7. Possible solution

Always capture metadata, not just links
A proper citation should survive even if the web page disappears.
Minimum useful metadata:
Author (or organization)
Title
Publication date
Publisher / journal / platform
Identifier (DOI, ISBN, etc.)
Access date (for unstable sources)
For example:
Smith, J. (2022). Title of Article. Journal Name. DOI: xxxx
Accessed: 2026-05-06
The access date matters specifically for unstable web content—it tells future readers what “version of reality” you saw.

---

#### 8. Conclusion: knowledge as sediment rather than structure

The intuition underlying your position is aligned with a growing recognition in information theory and epistemology: digital knowledge is not a stable edifice but a **sedimentary accumulation of partially preserved traces**.

Each layer of information is:

* partially derived
* partially lost
* partially reconstructed

The challenge is not to restore an illusion of perfect sourcing, but to design epistemic habits that remain meaningful under conditions of impermanence.
